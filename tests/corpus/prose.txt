Cities rarely grow according to a single plan. In most cases they expand outward in waves, and as a
result the oldest neighborhoods end up surrounded by newer ones. For example, a district that began as
farmland on the edge of town may, over time, become a dense residential core. On the other hand, the
original center often keeps its narrow streets long after the reason for them has disappeared. In other
words, the shape of a city is a record of decisions made for reasons that no longer apply.

This matters because planners have to work with what already exists. They cannot start from scratch, so
in order to improve traffic flow they have to thread new roads through old patterns. It is worth noting
that the most expensive mistakes tend to be the ones that are hardest to undo. A subway line, for
instance, is essentially permanent, and as a consequence the route chosen today will shape the city for
a century or more. Keep in mind that the people who benefit most from such projects are often not yet
born when the decision is made.

There is a lot of debate about how much planning is too much. Some argue that, in general, cities work
best when they are allowed to evolve on their own. Others point out that, without coordination, growth
tends to produce sprawl, congestion, and inequality. The difference between these two views comes down
to how much weight you give to individual choice compared to collective outcomes. In terms of results,
neither pure planning nor pure spontaneity has a clean track record.

What seems to work, based on the evidence, is a mix. Make sure the basic framework is in place, such as
water, power, and transit, and then let the details fill in over time. According to this view, the role
of the planner is not to dictate every outcome but to set the conditions under which good outcomes
become more likely. This means that the most important decisions are often the least visible ones. At
the same time, it is easy to underestimate how much a single bad rule can distort a city for decades.

In the end, the lesson is that cities are not machines to be designed but systems to be cultivated. As
discussed above, the patterns we see today are the accumulated result of countless small choices. Going
forward, the challenge is to make those choices a little more deliberate without pretending that anyone
can see the whole picture in advance.
